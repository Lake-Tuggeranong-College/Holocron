Have you ever started playing a mobile game, a simple arcade game, or an indie game on your phone, only to realize you’ve been tapping away for 20 minutes straight?

That happens because of a hidden design trick called the **Primary Gameplay Loop** (or "Core Loop"). A loop is a simple cycle of actions that a player does over and over again. When you are making smaller-scale games, nailing this loop is the most important step. If your core loop is fun, your whole game will be fun!

Academic researchers who study games, like Dr. Emmanuel Guardiola, point out that games are essentially built on these repeating loops of action and learning. Even in a tiny game, players are constantly trying an action, seeing what happens, and getting better at it.

## The 3 Pillars of a Gameplay Loop

Every solid gameplay loop is made of three basic parts that keep repeating:

Look at the diagram above. Notice how the arrows keep pulling you from one step to the next:

1. **The Objective (The Goal):** This tells you _where_ you need to go or what you need to do.
    
2. **The Challenge (The Path):** The obstacles, enemies, or puzzles blocking you from reaching your goal.
    
3. **The Reward (The Prize):** What you get for beating the challenge, which prepares you for the _next_ goal.

## Small Games, Big Loops: Real Examples

You don't need a massive budget or a giant map to make a great loop. Some of the most addictive games have tiny loops that take less than 10 seconds to complete.

| **Game**        | **1. The Action**              | **2. The Challenge**                 | **3. The Reward / Feedback**              |
| --------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Flappy Bird** | Tap the screen to flap upwards | Avoid crashing into the green pipes  | Score goes up + a satisfying "ding" sound |
| **Pac-Man**     | Move around a small maze       | Avoid the four colorful ghosts       | Eat all the dots + clear the screen       |
| **Crossy Road** | Tap to hop forward             | Avoid getting hit by cars and trains | Earn coins to unlock cool new characters  |
## How to Design Your Small-Scale Loop

When making a short or small game, you want to focus heavily on the second-to-second experience. Here is how you can map it out:

1. **Pick One:

Decide on the **one single action** the player will do the most. Is it _Jumping_? _Dodging_? _Catching_? Keep it simple. If your game is about catching falling apples, your verb is _Catch_.

2. **Create a Simple Hazard:

Make that core verb tricky so the player has to try. If they are catching apples, maybe some bad rotten apples fall down that they have to _avoid_.

3. **Give Instant Juice (The Reward):

When the player does the action successfully, make it feel awesome. Give them a point on the screen, play a fun sound effect, or make a little particle explosion. This instant feedback tells the brain: _"You did it! Do it again!"_

4. **Make it Slightly Faster/Harder:**

To keep a small game from getting boring, make the loop repeat a little bit faster or harder as the score goes up. The apples fall faster, or more cars appear on the road.

## Break it down with _VERBS_

When designing your loop always refer to what the player is _doing_ with _VERBS_

At the end of the day a core loop is a simple diagram that shows what the player is doing. It should be very simple and not <b>overdesigned</b>.

![[primaryloopverbs.webp]]

When designing this you should be answering the following:

**What is the player _doing_?**
**What is _stopping_ them?**
**Why are they _doing_ it?**
